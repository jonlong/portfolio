The Criterion Collection

Stack

  • Laravel 5 (PHP 7) and PostgreSQL

  • Sass and vanilla JS, compiled with Gulp

  • Deployed on Heroku, S3, and RDS

  • Homestead (Vagrant/VirtualBox) development environment

Between 2017 and 2018, I worked as a full stack developer helping to launch and maintain the first redesign of criterion.com in over a decade.

I was part of a small team contracted to pick up where a prior agency had left off, and inherited a beautifully-designed set of static front end templates and a partially completed back end build.

We were hired to figure out what remained, create a transparent project management workflow that would shepherd the team through the process, and complete the outstanding development required for launch. After the site launched in May 2018, we continued to build and release new features.

My Role

My largest responsibilities revolved around the following areas:

  • Interviewing the Criterion team to get a solid understanding of each department's workflows, gathering requirements and creating scopes for major features that hadn't shipped yet, and aligning expectations with stakeholders
  • Working with the previous development team to understand the project's architecture, technical decisions, and outstanding tasks
  • Planning and configuring our team's development workflow / deployment pipeline
  • Planning and executing a migration of the existing site's database content to the new site's updated structure
  • Shipping major features including homepage CMS integration, high-traffic Flash Sale pages, order editing and cancellation, CSV reporting, URL migrations, and a whole bunch of front end bug fixes and enhancements.

Trusting the Process

The heart of this project was the team's effort to radically improve the development process, transparency, and communication with the client stakeholders, something that was especially crucial given our role as the second development team to join the project.

Scoping a project in motion is tricky. There's so much existing background, so many foundational decisions already made, and so much context that we can only understand as work progresses. In order to create accurate scopes, we'd need a system that allowed us to chart how much work we could complete in a given time period, identify and break up outstanding features into actionable tasks, and plan for the unexpected along the way.

An agile approach checked all these boxes, and also gave us the opportunity to include the Criterion team at a much closer level. The system hasn't changed much since we began: we employ weekly scrum planning, post-sprint demos, kanban-style issue tracking, and commitment to regular backlog grooming sessions with the Criterion team.

We support this from a development standpoint using a Git Flow approach paired with Heroku's (brilliant) Review Apps feature, allowing us to stand up live builds of every Pull Request for the Criterion team to review. Once they validate a set of acceptance criteria, each branch is staged for inclusion in a post-sprint release.

Without a strong project management process, creating a window into the team's progress and developing a shared understanding of this project's scope would be impossible. An agile approach, coupled with a high degree of sprint visibility and direct collaboration from the stakeholders, has been critical in collectively defining and understanding a shared vision of success.

Lessons

I am incredibly lucky to work alongside this team and the exceptional people at Criterion, who have been enthusastic and focused partners throughout this project. They have consistently shown me the dividends of including regular client feedback as a fundamental part of the development process.

Some of the big wins we've shared as a result:

  • No more big surprises. If the team runs into a particularly hairy issue or an increase in scope, our sprint planning board acts as a shared playbook that shows us how to proceed. Since they're directly involved in the planning process, the client stays informed and keeps a handle on how changes affect the project as a whole.
  • Scope discussions are easier. We can track exactly how much work is possible within a given sprint, and plan our weeks around that metric. As priorities shift, we have to work together to determine which tasks get bumped to subsequent sprints, and which features might need to be deprioritized in order to meet a deadline.
  • The client is able to plan external business decisions around the pace of development, since we now have a quantifiable view of effort and velocity.
  • Finally, the process is hardly something that runs itself. Strong project management is required to keep everyone on track, because commitment and consistency is what ultimately makes this approach effective and reliable.
